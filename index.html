How this sleep calculator works

Sleep isn't one long block — it runs in cycles of roughly 90 minutes, each moving through light sleep, deep sleep, and REM. Waking at the end of a cycle, when you're naturally in light sleep, feels easy. Waking in the middle of deep sleep is what causes that heavy, groggy, "hit by a truck" feeling — even after plenty of hours.

This calculator counts backward (or forward) in 90-minute cycles and adds the average 15 minutes it takes to fall asleep, then shows you the bedtimes or wake-up times that land between cycles. The green option is the sweet spot for most adults: 5–6 full cycles (7.5–9 hours).

How much sleep do I need?

  • Adults (18–64): 7–9 hours — 5 to 6 cycles.
  • Teens (14–17): 8–10 hours — 5 to 7 cycles.
  • School-age kids (6–13): 9–11 hours — 6 to 7 cycles.
  • Older adults (65+): 7–8 hours — 4 to 5 cycles.

Tips for falling asleep faster

The 15-minute default assumes typical sleep latency. If you routinely take longer, raise the number in Options — and try these: keep the bedroom cool (65–68°F / 18–20°C), stop caffeine 8+ hours before bed (our Caffeine Half-Life Calculator shows exactly how much is still in your system at bedtime), dim screens an hour before sleep, and keep the same wake-up time every day — even weekends. A consistent wake time is the single strongest lever for better sleep.

Is 6 hours of sleep enough?

Four complete cycles (6 hours) is the minimum that still respects cycle boundaries — you may feel alert after it, but it's below the 7-hour floor most adults need long-term. Use it for a crunch night, not a lifestyle. This tool is for general wellness information and isn't medical advice; talk to a doctor about persistent sleep problems.
